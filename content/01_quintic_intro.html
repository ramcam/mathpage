Quintic polynomials are rarely solvable

The insolvability of the quintic is a problem that has given me a fair amount of headache. Its proof by means of Galois theory is highly abstract and is usually exposed by concatenating several seeminly unmotivated steps. Here I will try to take you (with some effort on your side) through a path in which I hope will feel that one step unfolds naturally from the other. It will probably not be the fastest path, but will surely be more rewarding than trying to digest a polished exposition.

Be sure to check the list of requirements before embarking on this journey. You should have basic knowledge of

  • Complex numbers
  • Groups, subgroups and normal subgroups
  • Rings and fields
  • Polynomials over a field
  • Field extensions
  • Algebraic closures

Let's get started!

Informally, what we set out to prove is that

The zeros of quintic polynomials cannot always be written with a radical expression.

We are referring to quintic polynomials with coefficients in $ \Q $ here. A radical expression means combining elements in $ \Q $ with the usual operations of the field ($ + - \times \div$) and $ \sqrt[n]{\phantom{a}} $, like this $$ \frac{3\sqrt[7]{1-\sqrt[3]{-1}}}{11\sqrt[5]{8}}. $$

We know from high school that this is possible, since for the polynomial $ p(x) = ax^2 + bx + c $ the zeros $ z_1, z_2 $ are given by the quadratic formula $$ z_{1,2} = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}. $$

We also know (although we don't bother to write it here) that this is also possible for any polynomial of degree $3$ or $4$. We will pretend that we don't know what happens for degrees $ n \geq 5 $ but some intuition tells us that it is not possible.

Our first task will be to restate the Insolvability of the Quintic in terms of some property of polynomials. Ideally, this property shows up in polynomials of degree $ n \leq 4 $ but is not present for higher degrees. What could this property be?

You will recall that a field is closed under the operations $ + - \times \div$ (except division by zero). However, $n$-th roots might take us outside of $ \Q $. Take for example the polynomials $$ \begin{align} p(x) &= x^2 - 2 = (x+\sqrt{2})(x-\sqrt{2}), \\ q(x) &= x^2 +1 = (x+i)(x-i). \end{align} $$ Their coefficients belong to $ \Q $ but their zeros, $ \pm \sqrt{2} $ and $ \pm i $ respectively, do not. This observation leads us to our first question:

What is the smallest algebraic structure containing the zeros of some polynomial $p (x) $?

We know that $ \mathbb{C} $, the algebraic closure of $ \Q $, contains the zeros. However, we don't need the full algebraic closure to discuss a specific polynomial. We can work with something much smaller. If dealing with $ p(x) = x^2 - 2 $, it suffices with the set of all elements of the form $ a+b\sqrt{2} $, with $a,b \in \Q$. This set is closed under the field operations and contains all the zeros of $ p(x) $. The same can be said about $ a + bi $ and $ q(x) $. These sets are called splitting fields and they seem important enough to have their definition inside a box:

The splitting field $ K $ of a polynomial with coefficients in the base field $ F $ is the smallest field where the polynomial decomposes into linear factors.

Before we constructed the splitting fields of $ p $ and $ q $ by adding a new element ($ \sqrt{2}$ and $ i $ respectively) and all its multiples with coefficients in the base field. This is a procedure we can formalize. Given a field $ F $, we can extend it with a new element $ \alpha $ to get a larger field. We say $ F $ adjoined $ \alpha $ for this new field and write $ F (\alpha) $. In case this field extension contains all the zeros of some polynomial $ r(x) $ we say it is its splitting field.

We now know how to extend once. There is nothing keeping us from extending again. Taking $ F = \Q (\sqrt{2}) $ and adjoining $ \sqrt{3} $ we get $ K = F(\sqrt{3}) = \Q ( \sqrt{2} )( \sqrt{3}) = \Q (\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}) $. This new field $ K $ contains all zeros of $ q(x) = p(x)(x^2-3) $, but also all the zeros of $ r(x) = q(x)(x-a_1)...(x-a_n) $ where the $ a_i $ are in $ \Q $.

One more example. Take $ r(x) = x^3 -2 $. Its zeros are $$ \begin{align} z_1 &= \sqrt[3]{2}, \\ z_2 &= \sqrt[3]{2}e^{\frac{2\pi}{3}i}, \\ z_3 &= \sqrt[3]{2}e^{\frac{4\pi}{3}i}. \end{align} $$ What is its splitting field? The set $ \Q(\sqrt[3]{2}) $ of all elements $ a + b\sqrt[3]{2} + c\sqrt[3]{4}$ is not yet splitting.
