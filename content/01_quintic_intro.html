Quintic polynomials are rarely solvable

$ \newcommand{\Q}{\mathbb{Q}} $ The insolvability of the quintic is a problem that has given me a fair amount of headache. Its proof by means of Galois theory is highly abstract and is usually exposed by concatenating several seeminly unmotivated steps.

Here I will try to take you (with some effort from your side) through a path in which you feel that one step unfolds naturally from the other.

It will probably not be the fastest path, but will surely be more rewarding than trying to digest a polished exposition.

Be sure to check the list of requirements before embarking on this journey. You should have basic knowledge of

  • Rings and fields
  • Polynomials over a field
  • Field extensions
  • Algebraic closures

Informally, what we set out to prove is that

The zeros of quintic polynomials cannot always be written with a radical expression.

We are referring to quintic polynomials with coefficients in $ \Q $ here. A radical expression means combining elements in $ \Q $ with the usual operations of the field ($ + - \times \div$) and $ \sqrt[n]{\phantom{a}} $, like this $$ \frac{3\sqrt[7]{1-\sqrt[3]{-1}}}{11\sqrt[5]{8}}. $$

You will recall that a field is closed under the operations $ + - \times \div$ (except for dividing by zero). However, $n$-th roots might take us outside of $ \Q $. This first observation leads us to our first question:

What is the algebraic structure containing the zeros of some polynomial $p (x) $?

If you are familiar with the concept of algebraic closure then you have an answer. However, we don't need the full algebraic closure of $ \Q $, that is $ \mathbb{C} $, to discuss a specific polynomial. We can work with something much smaller. If dealing with $ p(x) = x^2 - 2 $ we just need to extend $ \Q $ with $ \sqrt{2} $. We say $ \Q $ adjoined $ \sqrt{2} $ for this new field consisting of all elements of the form $ a+b\sqrt{2} $, and write $ \Q (\sqrt{2}) $. This new field contains all zeros of $ p(x) $.

There is nothing keeping us from extending again. Taking $ F = \Q (\sqrt{2}) $ and adjoining $ \sqrt{3} $ we get $ K = F(\sqrt{3}) = \Q ( \sqrt{2} )( \sqrt{3}) = \Q (\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}) $. This new field $ K $ contains all zeros of $ q(x) = p(x)(x^2-3) $, but also all the zeros of $ r(x) = q(x)(x-a_1)...(x-a_n) $ where the $ a_i $ are in $ \Q $. Let's call this field, the smallest one containing all the zeros of some $ p(x) $, the splitting field of $ p(x) $.
