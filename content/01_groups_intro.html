What is a group?

You probably heard that wherever symmetry appears, groups appear too. Maybe you heard that they have to do with solving equations. Both are true. But also, much more. Groups are rather simple, yet a very rich theory emerges from them connecting many distant areas in math. What are groups, why are they interesting and in which context do they arise naturally?

First, an informal definition:

A group is the simplest algebraic structure where some operation can always be undone.

What do we mean by this?

Suppose you are working on a problem involving some set of mathematical objects which can be combined to produce objects of the same kind. We will look at specific examples in the next section. For the moment, let's say that $ a, b $ are elements you found inside some set $ X $, and you have a way of combining them to produce some element $ c $ also in $ X $. Let's call this a binary operation without going into much detail, and write "$ + $" for this operation, so $ a + b = c$. We could also write "$ * $" so $ a * b = c $, or just $ ab = c $. It is up to you which symbol to use, although it is usual to use the additive notation $ a + b $ for commutative operations and the multiplicative notation $ ab $ for non-commutative operations. We will use multiplicative notation for the rest of this section.

Suppose now that in your quest to solve your problem, you arrive at some expression $ ax= b $ (or $ a+x=b $ in additive notation), where $ x $ is unknown.

Take a minute to observe the equation $$ ax = b. $$ It's a really minimal expression involving very few elements. It should be really easy to solve, right? We have $ a$ and $ b $, two known objects, and one third unknown object $ x $ stuck to the right of the $ a $ by one implicit operation.

You maybe wonder if it would be possible to neutralize that annoying $ a $ so the $ x $ is isolated, and the problem solved.

What should we require for that to be possible?

Certainly if in the set of objects where we found $ a $ we require that for any given object we can find an inverse, then we have come a long way. Let's call it $ a^{-1} $ or $ -a $ in additive notation.

We can apply the inverse to both sides of $ ax = b$ and move a few steps towards our goal: $$ \begin{align} ax &= b\\ a^{-1}(ax) &= a^{-1}(b) \\ (a^{-1}a)x&= a^{-1}b. \end{align} $$ For the last step we have implicitly introduced a new requirement: our operations must be associative so we can place the parentheses around the "$ a $"s.

We are now very close to solving for $ x $. We just declared that we must always be able to find inverses, but we said nothing about what an inverse is, or what happens when operating something with its inverse. We need that $ a^{-1} a $ somehow vanishes. Here comes the last requirement: $ a^{-1} a $ should result in an object, let's name it $ e $, with special properties. This is the special element $ e $ that combined with any other element $ g $ results again in $ g $. This must be true whem applying $ e $ to the left or right of $ g $. Then $ eg=ge = g $. This is the element that does nothing. You can think of it as vanishing. We call $ e $ the identity element.

We can finally solve for $ x $, using that $ a^{-1}a=e $: $$ \begin{align} (a^{-1}a)x&= a^{-1}b \\ ex&= a^{-1}b \\ x&= a^{-1}b. \end{align} $$ We have found one solution for $ x $! To guarantee this required the existence of inverse elements, an associative operation and an identity element.

These requirements are what define a group structure. We are now ready for the formal definition of a group:

A group is a set $ G $ closed under an associative binary operation $ * $ such that:
  • There is an $ e \in G $ such that $ g*e = e*g = g $ for every $ g \in G $.
  • For every $ g \in G $ there is a $ g^{-1} $ such that $ g^{-1}*g = g*g^{-1} = e $.

We found one solution by imposing a group structure. But.. are there more?

Let's give this a thought. Suppose there is more than one solution. That is, there is $ y \neq x $ such that $ ay = b $. Then, solving for $ y $ like we did before for $ x$ we have $ y = a^{-1}b $. But $ x = a^{-1}b $ too, so $ a^{-1}b \neq a^{-1}b $ which is a contradiction. It turns out a group structure guarantees the existence of a unique solution. As we mentioned in our informal definition, this is the smallest structure achieving this. Not bad for such a short list of requirements!

Take a moment again to think about what we just achieved. What kind of objects and operations can be regarded as groups? Some examples are addition of integers, addition of vectors, composition of invertible functions, multiplication of invertible matrices, chaining rotations in space, chaining symmetries of polyhedra, chaining permutations and much more.
