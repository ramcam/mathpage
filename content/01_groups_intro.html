What is a group?

Suppose you find yourself working with a problem. Suppose this problem involves some set of mathematical objects which can be combined to produce objects of the same kind.

Maybe you are adding integer numbers, or combining rotations and reflections that leave the vertices of some shape fixed, maybe you are just performing rotations one ofter another in space, maybe you are composing continuous functions, or adding vectors, or just adding minutes in a clock while you wait for the bus. It doesn't matter what the objects are as long as two of them combined produce a third of the same kind. We will be more specific about what that might mean.

If you were adding integers $ a $ and $ b $, you would surely write $ a + b = c $. When combining two rotations $ A $ and $ B $ in space, maybe $ AB = C $ is convenient since $ AB \neq BA $ in three dimensions, and using $ + $ would be confusing. If composing functions $ a(x) $ and $ b(x) $ then $ a \circ b = c$ is standard notation. For vector addition or minutes in a clock $ a + b = c $ should also make sense. Again, it is irrelevant which symbol you choose for the binary operation at hand. It is however usual to use the additive notation $ a + b $ for commutative operations and the multiplicative notation $ ab $ for non-commutative operations.

Suppose now that in your quest to solve your problem, you arrive at some expression $ ax= b $ (or $ a+x=c $ ), where $ x $ is unknown. You wonder if it would be possible to neutralize that annoying $ a $ so the $ x $ is isolated, and the problem solved.

What should we require for that to be possible?

Certainly if in the set of objects, for any given $ a $ we can find an inverse object, we have come a long way. Let's call it $ a^{-1} $ or $ -a $ in additive notation.

This allows us to do apply the inverse to both sides of the equation $$ \begin{align} ax &= b\\ a^{-1}(ax) &= a^{-1}(b) \\ (a^{-1}a)x&= a^{-1}b. \end{align} $$ Note that we have introduced a new requirement in the last step: our operations must be associative so we can place the parentheses around the "$ a $"s. We are very close to solving for $ x $. We now need that $ a^{-1} a $ somehow vanishes. We must require that the result of $ a^{-1} a $ is a special object, let's name it $ e $, with the property that $ e $ combined with any other element $ g $ results again in $ g $. This must be true whem applying $ e $ to the left or right of $ g $. Then $ eg=ge = g $. We call $ e $ the identity element.

We can pick up where we left it and finally solve for $ x $: $$ \begin{align} (a^{-1}a)x&= a^{-1}b \\ ex&= a^{-1}b \\ x&= a^{-1}b. \end{align} $$ We have found one solution for $ x $! To guarantee this we had to require:

  • Existence of inverse elements
  • Associativity
  • Identity element.
These requirements are what define a group structure. We are now ready for the formal definition of a group:

A group is a set $ G $ closed under an associative binary operation $ * $ such that:
  • There is an $ e \in G $ such that $ g*e = e*g = g $ for every $ g \in G $.
  • For every $ g \in G $ there is a $ g^{-1} $ such that $ g^{-1}*g = g*g^{-1} = e $.

We have found one solution by imposing a group structure. But.. are there more?

Let's give this a thought. Suppose there is more than one solution. That is, there is $ y \neq x $ such that $ ay = b $. Then, solving for $ y $ like we did before for $ x$ we have $ y = a^{-1}b $. But $ x = a^{-1}b $ too, so $ a^{-1}b \neq a^{-1}b $ which is a contradiction. It turns out a group structure guarantees the existence of a unique solution. Not bad for such a short list of requirements!

What kind of problems do not accept a group structure? Many kinds, for example problems involving irreversible operations. Projecting a 3d sphere into a disk is irreversible because if you were handed the squished disk in the first place, how could you tell if its original state was a sphere or a cylinder? Similarly, multiplying an integer by zero is irreversible. What could you possibly do to zero to get the original integer? A more informal, but maybe more insightful definition of what a group is could be the following:

A group is an algebraic structure where operations can always be undone.
