What is a group?

Informally, a group is an algebraic structure where operations can always be undone.

Take a binary operation $ * $ and a set $ G $ closed respect to $ * $. Any two elements $ g, h \in G $ can be combined to produce a third like so: $$ g*h = k. $$ You may think of this as something we did to $ h $ on its right side.

Since we said every operation in our group may be undone, it should be possible to write $$ g^{-1}*k = g^{-1}*g*h = h $$ to obtain the original $h$. In order for this to work properly, we need that $ g^{-1}*g $ equals something that leaves $ h $ untoched. We call this the identity element $ e $. Then $$ g^{-1}*k = g^{-1}*g*h = e*h = h. $$ Why is this interesting? A couple of reasons: You probably heard that wherever there is symmetry, there is a group. Also, the simplest equation you can imagine, $ ax = b $ requires you to use groups and nothing more in order to solve it.

Here is the formal definition of a group:

A group is a set $ G $ closed under the binary operation $ * $ such that:
  • There is an $ e \in G $ such that $ g*e = e*g = g $ for every $ g \in G $.
  • For every $ g \in G $ there is a $ g^{-1} $ such that $ g*g^{-1} = g^{-1}*g = e $.
  • The binary operation is associative, that is, for $ g, h, j \in G $, $ (g*h)*j = g*(h*j) $.
